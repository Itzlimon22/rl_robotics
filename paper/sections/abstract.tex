\begin{abstract}
Classical control of autonomous underwater vehicles (AUVs) fails
under ocean-realistic physics variation: a PID controller achieving
$100\%$ success under nominal conditions collapses to $3\%$ when
drag and current shift to real-ocean ranges — a $97$-percentage-point
collapse that motivates physics-aware training.
We present the first systematic study of \emph{Curriculum Domain
Randomisation} (\CDR{}) for AUV fluid physics: a performance-gated
strategy that expands six randomisable physics parameters during training
based on a rolling success rate window.
Compared against Uniform DR, Naive SAC, and a PID baseline across nine
experiments ($3$ conditions $\times$ $3$ seeds $\times$ $10^6$ steps),
\CDR{} achieves $[FILL:sr_cdr] \pm [FILL:std_cdr]$\% transfer success
on a held-out test distribution while consuming $[FILL:saving]\%$ less
thrust energy per step than Uniform DR
($[FILL:eng_cdr] \pm [FILL:engstd_cdr]$ vs
$[FILL:eng_udr] \pm [FILL:engstd_udr]$,
$p = [FILL:pval]$, $d = [FILL:cohend]$),
a meaningful saving for battery-constrained real AUV missions.
Domain randomisation eliminates the $\pm [FILL:std_naive]$\%
deployment variance of policies trained without randomisation.
We open-source our MuJoCo AUV environment with nine randomisable
parameters to facilitate reproducible underwater robotics research.
\end{abstract}
